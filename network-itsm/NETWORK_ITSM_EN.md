# Network and IT Service Management

## 1. Introduction: The Need for Management

A computer network is the nervous system of modern business. **Network management** is the process of operating and controlling it so that it works properly and provides real value.

Without a management plan, IT teams act in a reactive "firefighting" mode—dealing with breakdowns and immediate problems instead of proactively improving the system. Network management covers four main areas:

- **Configuration:** Managing devices and software.
- **Performance & Faults:** Keeping it fast and fixing breakdowns.
- **End User Support:** Helping employees.
- **Cost:** Controlling the budget.

### The Shift Toward Integration

In the past, voice (telephones, managed by Facilities) and data (computers, managed by IT) were handled separately. Likewise, LANs and WANs were independent.

Today, organizations **integrate** voice, data, LANs, and WANs into a single system. This reduces costs, simplifies the network, and eliminates redundant departments. To succeed, managers must compromise on local policies in favor of a written charter and long-range organizational goals.

## 2. Configuration Management

You cannot manage what you don't know exists. Configuration management tracks hardware, software, and users, ensuring documentation is updated with every change.

### Managing Users and Software

When a new user joins, they are placed in a group with specific privileges and given a **login script** (commands automatically executed at login to map drives or printers).

Instead of updating software manually, managers use **Electronic Software Distribution (ESD)** to push updates automatically over the network at scheduled times. This reduces long-term costs and produces accurate reports. The **Desktop Management Interface (DMI)** provides a standard framework for managing these remote configurations.

### Documentation: The Network's Map

Accurate documentation is crucial for troubleshooting and planning. It should include:

- **Hardware:** Network diagrams (mapping circuits and devices), serial numbers, vendors, purchase dates, warranty info, and repair contacts.
- **Software:** Network operating systems, version releases, and site licenses.
- **Profiles:** User and application file access rights.
- **Guidelines:** Procedures for performance, maintenance, and Disaster Recovery Plans (DRP).

## 3. Performance and Fault Management

### Network Monitoring

**Performance management** ensures efficiency, while **fault management** prevents, detects, and corrects breakdowns. Both rely heavily on continuous **network monitoring**.

A **Network Operations Center (NOC)** is a centralized facility where managers use specialized software to monitor the network and fix issues before users even notice. They collect:

- **Physical statistics:** Hardware data from modems, multiplexers, and circuits.
- **Logical information:** User response times, traffic volumes, and service-level indicators.

### The Failure Control Function

When faults occur, they are tracked via **Trouble Tickets**—digital reports generated by a central help desk or automatically triggered by smart devices experiencing internal failures.

Tickets serve four core purposes:

1. **Problem Tracking:** Assigning personnel and tracking resolution timelines.
2. **Problem Statistics:** Analyzing which hardware or circuits fail most often.
3. **Problem Prioritization:** Ensuring critical issues are fixed first.
4. **Management Reporting:** Evaluating network availability and vendor responsiveness.

Tickets are compiled into a **Trouble Log** to identify recurring patterns (e.g., recognizing that out of 88 calls, 49 were about client computers while only 2 were about external carriers).

### Measuring Reliability: Key Statistics

Managers rely on specific metrics to understand network health:

- **Availability:** The percentage of time the network is fully operational (Target: 99% - 99.5%).
  _Calculation: Hours available ÷ 720 monthly hours._
- **Downtime:** The exact opposite; the percentage of time the network is unavailable.
- **MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures):** The hours or days of continuous operation before a breakdown. A high MTBF indicates excellent component reliability.
- **MTTR (Mean Time To Repair):** Average time to restore operation. It combines three phases:
  1. **MTTDiagnose:** Finding the root cause (indicates NOC efficiency).
  2. **MTTRespond:** Waiting for the vendor or technician to arrive.
  3. **MTTFix:** Actually resolving the issue (indicates repair efficiency).

Daily statistics (transmission volume, capacity utilization, error rates) are plotted on **Quality Control Charts** against upper and lower acceptable limits to predict failures and plan necessary hardware upgrades.

### Improving Performance Under Pressure

When the network is congested, managers use **Policy-Based Management** to enforce software-defined priorities. For example, using TCP/IP QoS to prioritize live videoconferencing traffic, because a delay will ruin a video call but won't be noticed in a file download.

Service providers are held accountable through **Service Level Agreements (SLAs)**—formal contracts defining acceptable performance (e.g., 99% availability, <2 minutes response time, MTBF >120 days) and compensation if those standards are not met.

## 4. End User Support

End user support exists to help employees solve problems. Issues usually fall into three categories:

1. **Hardware failures:** (e.g., broken monitors) Usually the easiest to fix.
2. **Lack of knowledge:** Often solved through patient, step-by-step guidance.
3. **Software problems:** Typically the hardest to resolve, involving hidden bugs or incompatible settings.

### The Resolution Process

When a user requests help, a trouble ticket is created. Resolution happens in tiers:

- **Level 1 (Help Desk):** Solves ~85% of common everyday issues.
- **Level 2 (Specialized Staff):** Handles problems requiring specific, deeper expertise.
- **Level 3 (Technical Specialists):** Fixes highly complex system issues or works directly with vendors.

### Continuous Training

Training is an ongoing responsibility because employees constantly change roles and software updates. Training includes in-class sessions, personalized one-on-one instruction, and detailed training manuals.

## 5. Cost Management

Managing costs is notoriously difficult because network traffic always outgrows budgets.

### TCO vs. NCO

- **Total Cost of Ownership (TCO):** Measures the true annual cost of a computer (estimated at $5,000–$10,000/year). It includes hardware, software, support, training, and crucially, the cost of **wasted user productivity** when systems break.
- **Net Cost of Ownership (NCO):** Often called the "Real TCO" ($1,500–$3,500/year). It only measures direct hard costs and explicitly excludes estimated wasted user time.

### Where Does the Budget Go?

For a typical 100-user network costing $150,000–$350,000 annually, the largest expenses are:

1. **Personnel costs (50–70% of the total budget)**
2. WAN communication circuits
3. Hardware upgrades and replacements

**Key Insight:** Because personnel is the biggest expense, organizations must focus on saving IT staff time rather than merely buying cheaper hardware.

### Reducing Costs

- **Standardization:** Using standard hardware/software makes troubleshooting and training significantly faster.
- **Automation:** Using ESD (for software) and DHCP (for IP addresses) eliminates hundreds of hours of manual labor.
- **Centralized Help Desks:** Grouping specialists in one location speeds up problem resolution.
- **Thin Clients:** Lightweight, stripped-down computers reduce software licensing and local support costs.
- **Charge-Back Policies:** Departments pay IT directly for their specific network usage. (This is common for easily measured WAN/Mainframe usage, but harder to implement for LANs).

## 6. Incident Management & ITSM

### Incident Management

While fault management handles daily breakdowns (like a jammed printer), **Incident Management** deals with disasters (like major technical failures, security breaches, or natural disasters).

**Primary Goal:** Minimize business impact and restore normal operations as quickly as possible.

A successful response relies on 5 key elements:

1. **Detection and Identification**
2. **Assessment and Prioritization**
3. **Response**
4. **Resolution and Recovery**
5. **Documentation and Learning**

### IT Service Management (ITSM)

Incident management is part of **ITSM**, a philosophy that treats IT not just as "managing computers," but as "delivering services" to the business.

The core ITSM lifecycle includes:

- **Service Strategy**
- **Service Design**
- **Service Transition**
- **Service Operation**
- **Continual Service Improvement (CSI)**

Instead of inventing management rules from scratch, organizations follow established ITSM frameworks like **ITIL**, **COBIT**, and **ISO/IEC 20000**. These frameworks help organizations align IT services with business needs, increase operational efficiency, ensure regulatory compliance, and ultimately support business innovation.
